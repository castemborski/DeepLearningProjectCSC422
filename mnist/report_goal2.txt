1. OVERVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
This report describes the implementation and evaluation of the Fast Gradient Sign
Method (FGSM) adversarial attack on the best-performing CNN from Goal 1 (batch
size=64, lr=1.0, 99.15% test accuracy on MNIST). FGSM is a one-step gradient-
based white-box attack that constructs adversarial examples — imperceptibly
perturbed inputs that cause a trained model to misclassify — by moving each input
pixel in the direction that maximally increases the loss.

2. METHODOLOGY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.1  Best Model Selection

The model chosen for attack is the CNN from Goal 1 (mnist_cnn_best.pt): two
convolutional layers (32 and 64 filters, kernel=3) followed by MaxPool, dropout,
and two fully connected layers (9216→128→10). It achieves 99.15% test accuracy
under no attack (epsilon=0), confirming it as the strongest checkpoint.

2.2  FGSM Attack

Given a correctly normalized input image x, its true label y, and the trained
model f parameterized by weights θ, the adversarial example is:

    x_adv = clip(x + ε · sign(∇_x L(f(x; θ), y)), x_min, x_max)

where L is the NLL loss, ε is the attack budget (perturbation magnitude), and the
clip ensures the perturbed image stays within the valid normalized pixel range for
MNIST ([–0.4242, 2.8215], corresponding to original pixel values in [0, 1] with
mean=0.1307 and std=0.3081). The sign of the gradient points in the direction that
increases the loss fastest; even a tiny step of ε in that direction can flip the
model's prediction.

The attack is applied to all 10,000 MNIST test images at seven epsilon levels:
ε ∈ {0, 0.05, 0.10, 0.15, 0.20, 0.25, 0.30}. For each image the loss is
computed, backpropagated to the input, and the signed gradient is used to create
the adversarial example. The model is then re-evaluated on the perturbed inputs.

4. ANALYSIS AND KEY FINDINGS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4.1  Monotone accuracy degradation

Model accuracy decreases monotonically as ε increases, falling from 99.15% at
ε=0 to 90.46% at ε=0.30 — a total drop of 8.69 percentage points. The drop
accelerates at higher epsilon: the step from ε=0.25 to ε=0.30 alone removes
251 additional correct predictions.

4.2  High robustness relative to the standard FGSM benchmark

The PyTorch FGSM tutorial reports that a similar MNIST CNN collapses to roughly
33% accuracy at ε=0.30. Our model retains 90.46%, indicating substantially
higher adversarial robustness. Two factors contribute:

  (a) Dropout regularization (p=0.25 and p=0.50) smooths the loss landscape,
      reducing the magnitude and consistency of input-space gradients and
      making a single gradient step less effective at crossing a decision
      boundary.

  (b) High clean accuracy (99.15% vs. ~98% in the tutorial) leaves a smaller
      margin for the attack to exploit; already near-optimal decisions require
      a larger perturbation to flip.

4.3  FGSM attack limitations

FGSM is a single-step, untargeted attack and is known to be among the weakest
adversarial attacks available. Its effectiveness is bounded by the loss gradient
at the clean input, which may not point toward the nearest decision boundary. More
powerful iterative attacks (PGD, C&W) perform multiple gradient steps or optimize
directly for misclassification distance and would achieve far larger accuracy
drops at the same ε budget.

4.4  Human-imperceptible perturbations cause measurable degradation

Even at ε=0.05, where the adversarial images are visually indistinguishable from
the originals, the attack already degrades accuracy by 0.73 percentage points
(73 additional misclassifications). This demonstrates the core vulnerability of
gradient-based models: small, structured perturbations that are invisible to
humans can shift model confidence enough to change predictions.

5. CONCLUSION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
This experiment demonstrates that FGSM adversarial attacks are effective against
the MNIST CNN even at small perturbation budgets. The best model from Goal 1
shows robustness that exceeds the standard FGSM benchmark, likely attributable to
dropout regularization and high clean accuracy; nevertheless, accuracy degrades
consistently and measurably across all ε values tested. The results illustrate
both the practical risk of adversarial examples — small, imperceptible changes
that cause misclassification — and the need for explicit adversarial training or
certified defenses to close the remaining 8.69-point gap between clean and
adversarial performance.
